The 7 easiest languages to learn

Publish date: 2024-07-09

Learning a language becomes easier the more languages you already know. But you have to start somewhere. Don’t block yourself by picking a language that is particularly difficult to acquire for English speakers as your next one. Instead, turn language learning into a self-fulfilling prophecy by focusing first on those you’ll be able to master quickly.

Which are the easiest languages to learn for English speakers?

English is a very connected language: it has links to many European Germanic languages and more than 50 percent of English words have Latin or French roots. The structure, alphabet and makeup of English are similar to French, Italian, Spanish and other Latin languages, giving you as an English speaker a head start when learning these languages.

1. Spanish

Acquiring Spanish is easy to very easy for English speakers. The orthography is shallow, meaning many words are written as pronounced. English and Spanish share Latin roots, so you’ll find many cognates in the vocabulary. Spanish has only ten vowels and diphthongs compared to 20 in English; pronunciation as well as reading and writing should come easy to you.

2. Italian 

Italian is a Romance language of the Indo-European family. Thanks to its Latin roots, you’ll find many Italian-English cognates. The consonants and vowels of the language are among the most common sounds in the world’s languages, making pronunciation fairly easy. You’ll also find the rhythm of speaking Italian easy to mimic.

3. French 

On the surface, French and English are very different, but most of that appearance is owed to pronunciation. This will be the hardest part of learning French for English speakers. Dig deeper and you find that French has influenced English a lot: both languages share words stemming from Latin.

4. Dutch

Dutch is a cousin of English, so to speak: it’s the third most-spoken Germanic language, sharing a lot of words with English. In fact, many of those are spelled identically with only a difference in pronunciation. However, the many cognates are also accompanied by a few false friends.

5. Swedish

You’ve probably guessed it: Swedish also has many words that stem from the same root as English. A simple grammar with similar word order makes Swedish easy to acquire for English speakers. A huge advantage is the mainly constant verb forms in Swedish, so unlike in Latin languages, you don’t conjugate as much!

6. Portuguese

After Spanish, you should be able to catch the drift of Portuguese rather quickly as an English speaker. The tricky part is the pronunciation, so you’ll have to train your ear a bit. But Portuguese has a nice rhythm to follow. Shared vocabulary will help you pick up the language and the huge possibility for exposure should serve you as well.

7. Norwegian

You might not have guessed this one, yet Norwegian and English are both Germanic languages and therefore share some vocabulary. The grammar is easy to acquire thanks to minimal conjugation and the word order in Norwegian is the same as in English. Pronunciation appears tricky at first, but keep in mind that Norway has many accents – although you may sound different than the natives, you’ll be understood nonetheless!

What makes a language easy to learn?

There are several factors that contribute to how easy you’ll find learning a new language:

  • Native language: your mother tongue remains your reference point and will always draw comparisons with the foreign language you’re trying to acquire. Similarities such as cognates and words with the same linguistic derivation, make it easier to learn vocabulary, which is the case with English speakers studying French.
  • Learning skills: a knack for understanding grammar, a mind for memorizing vocabulary, a photographic memory for written characters, or a talent for mimicking pronunciation certainly help. Choose the fast track and pick a language that plays to your particular skills.
  • Exposure: as children growing up bilingual demonstrate, exposure to a foreign language (especially at an early age) enables you to pick up a lot.
  • Grammar: a familiar sentence structure and word order allow you to draw parallels to your native language – or another language in case you’re multilingual.
  • Pronunciation: language is communication, so speaking is literally half the work of mastering a language. A pronunciation that plays to your skills or is close to what’s already familiar will boost your language learning.

Pick your language and start learning today

Now that you discovered the easiest languages available for English speakers to learn, it is time to take the first step in this beautiful journey. It will require a little courage and some focus. Also, you will have to be patient and stay motivated. If you’re reluctant, just keep in mind that nothing comes easy. So, always remember why you’re learning a new language and stay focused!
